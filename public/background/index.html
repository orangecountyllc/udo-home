The Story Behind Birdy

    It all started when I moved to a big metro city and, for the first time in my life, realized I didn’t need my car. Between the traffic, endless parking headaches, and a city that seemed to invent new ways to fine me, I had enough. I sold my car and turned to rideshare apps — thinking, “This will be easier.”

    At first, it was. But the more I used them, the more I saw cracks in the system. I grew up in California where my uncle Tony runs a small limo business. He’s a happy man — his clients know him, trust him, and he gets paid fairly for the work he does. But what I saw in the world of rideshare couldn’t have been more different.

    As a rider, I noticed something off. Drivers seemed burnt out. The fares were high, but the people doing the actual work — the drivers — weren’t seeing much of that money. So I dug deeper. I rode more. Talked to more drivers. And what I learned stuck with me.

    The truth? Many drivers are barely getting by. They’re working long hours, juggling multiple apps, and still struggling. Most riders don’t realize that their $20 ride might leave a driver with less than $10. Meanwhile, the relationship between rider and driver is totally transactional — no connection, no loyalty, no real trust.

    But it doesn’t have to be that way.

    What I found is that private drivers — like limo drivers or long-time drivers — live a different reality. Their clients know them. Trust them. Tip them directly. There’s a real relationship. And with that comes better service, safety, and comfort — on both sides.

    I also saw how much safety matters, especially to women. Some shared stories of rude or sketchy drivers. Many didn’t feel safe giving out their number, even when they wanted to reconnect with a driver they actually liked. That stuck with me.

    One day, a driver friend in his 60s told me, “My client wants to tip me, but I don’t know how to get paid without cash.” He didn’t have Zelle. I realized most of the tools people needed just didn’t exist. That’s when the lightbulb went off.

    What if we gave drivers a digital business card — one that let riders reconnect with them, follow their location, and build real trust over time?

    What if we made it easy for people to choose their driver — someone they already know?

    From that idea, Birdy was born. With Birdy, drivers can create a profile, share their real story, and let riders know where they are and when they’re available. Riders, in turn, can find someone they trust — not just someone the algorithm throws at them. It’s about familiarity, fairness, and freedom. Because let’s face it: if you’ve already spent 20 minutes in a car with someone and it went well, wouldn’t you want to ride with them again?



    Why “Birdy”?

    At first, I thought about calling it Freedom — because that’s what this is really about. Freedom from low pay. Freedom from random strangers. Freedom from anxiety.

    But “Freedom App” didn’t roll off the tongue. It felt too heavy, too political. So I kept brainstorming.

    I landed on Free Birds — then just Bird. Like a helicopter, fast and light. People I spoke with said “Birdy” felt warm, easy, and fun. And when someone said, “Birdie sounds like someone I’d want to ride with,” — I knew I had my name.



    Birdy is about human connections. No middlemen. No noise. Just real people helping real people get around — and get paid. If you’ve ever taken a rideshare and thought, “I wish I could ride with that same driver again,” — Birdy is for you.

    Let’s ride differently. Let’s ride with Birdy. – Jay jay@birdyone.com
